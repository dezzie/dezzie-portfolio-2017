EDGAR

Simple prototyping to design a complex experience.

Dates
2015
Role
User Experience
Organization
Securities & Exchange Commission (SEC)

The SEC's mission is to promote fairness in markets, striking a balance between protecting investors and encouraging capital formation.

EDGAR is a database that allows anyone to access real-time information about publicly traded companies. From mundane filings about a company's quarterly earnings to juicy paper trails about your favorite tycoon's insider transactions, EDGAR offers a wealth of information for investment experts to investigative journalists alike (pun intended).

I took a slippy UX approach to redesigning the EDGAR interface, one that would allow users to focus on getting what they needed and nothing else. They've got more important things to do.

Paper prototyping a search interface

Working with a new team at CHIEF HQ, some of my colleagues hadn't worked with robust browser applications before. I turned to paper prototypes to quickly convey initial directions and work out any major, unseen dead ends together.

Architecting the interface

EDGAR's landing page currently lacks a meaningful introduction describing what it is, and the things users can do with it.

WHY DID YOU CHANGE IT?!

A simple onboarding experience provides all the information users need to know to get back to work using the new EDGAR. Curently, new users must download and search through a Word document to get started. My feelings on the rampant use of type of documentation in the government have been previously expressed.

Search results

A simpler, cleaner, search results view demonstrates information architecture decisions aimed at reducing cognitive overload.

Filtering

The UI compartmentalizes tasks in the search process to improve focus, not break it.

Connecting the dots

Facets are different from filters in that they allow users to find connections between companies and people. Whereas Filters are used to narrow down a large number of results, users are in sleuth mode when using Facets.

Viewing the details

This is what a typical SEC filing document looks like. Taking a cue from material design, the UI helps users focus on scanning a filing, but does not break their flow, since they may not find what they're looking for the right time.

EDGAR's redesign is in the early stages of development. Here's why I chose to design 2 directions:

In the past, unprecedented government-specific quirks have resulted in major parts of a tool's architecture to drastically change, undermining the designer's intent long after she can do anything about it. Providing a backup plan ensures that, no matter what happens, the SEC team can get it right the first time.
